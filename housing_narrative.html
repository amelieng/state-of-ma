Economic Stability · Housing

Boston can't afford
to stay in Boston.

A data narrative in seven chapters. We follow the widening gap between what Boston earns and what Boston costs — from the moment a household tries to buy a home, to the generations locked out of ownership, to the supply shortage that made it all worse.

Chapter 1 Available

The Boston median income can no longer buy a Boston home.

A household earning Boston's median income would need to nearly double it — to $179K — just to qualify for a mortgage on a median-priced home today. This chapter walks through the year-by-year collapse of affordability from 2005 to 2024, lets you see where your own occupation falls, and identifies the two worst turning points in the data.

Most Bostonians aren't building wealth —
they're paying someone else's mortgage.

68% of Boston residents rent. This chapter shows the long-run shift from ownership to renting, tracks rent burden as a share of income over time, and makes the wealth transfer visible: every month of rent is equity someone else accumulates.

Data needed for this chapter:

  • Median gross rent as % of median household income — homeownership_summary.csv (column: rent_burden_pct_of_income)
  • Key stat: 68% of Boston residents rent (ACS B25003, most recent year)

A Boston first-time buyer today is
10 years older than their parents were.

The age at which Bostonians buy their first home has shifted dramatically. A generation of potential buyers is priced out or delayed — not because they're irresponsible, but because the math no longer works.

Boston has been underbuilding for decades.

The city permitted fewer homes in 2024 than it did in 1985. This chapter maps the supply gap — the cumulative shortfall between housing demand and housing built — and traces how decades of underproduction made the affordability crisis structural, not cyclical.
